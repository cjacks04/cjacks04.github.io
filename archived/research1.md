<div class="callout-box">
  <strong>Research overview:</strong> My research examines how people learn, participate, and make decisions in data-rich and AI-mediated environments. Across projects, I study how non-experts develop meaningful expertise in complex socio-technical systems and how data-driven systems can better reflect public values, community knowledge, and fairness.
</div>

<h2>Research Approach</h2>
<p>
  I use mixed methods that combine computational analysis of digital trace data, qualitative and ethnographic inquiry, and community-engaged design. Across projects, I am interested in how people build expertise, how technical systems shape participation, and how communities can more meaningfully influence the design and governance of data and AI systems.
</p>

<h2>Current Research Projects</h2>

<div class="research-project">
  <div class="research-project-text">
    <h3>Gravity Spy 2.0</h3>
    <p>
      <strong>What it is:</strong> Gravity Spy 2.0 builds on our earlier citizen science work by asking whether volunteers can do more than simple classification. In this project, we support participants in identifying the likely causes of noise in the LIGO gravitational-wave detectors using auxiliary data, structured background knowledge, and interactive tools.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>Why it matters:</strong> This project explores how non-experts can be supported in doing forms of scientific reasoning that are usually reserved for specialists. It helps us understand what kinds of interfaces, explanations, and learning supports allow people to move from routine tasks to more advanced causal analysis.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>What we are building and studying:</strong> We are designing tools that give volunteers access to richer contextual information, including supporting data channels and ontology-based explanations, in order to test how people learn to reason through more complex scientific problems.
    </p>

<div class="research-project">
  <div class="research-project-text">
    <h3>Participatory AI Auditing</h3>
    <p>
      <strong>What it is:</strong> This project explores the role that ordinary people can play in evaluating AI systems used in public life. Rather than treating fairness as only a technical problem, this work studies how non-expert participants reason about context, trade-offs, and values when reviewing systems used in areas such as housing, policing, and public decision-making.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>Why it matters:</strong> Many forms of algorithmic harm are difficult to identify through technical metrics alone. This project asks what becomes visible when non-experts are treated not just as affected users, but as normative agents capable of identifying concerns and articulating competing ideas of fairness.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>What we are building and studying:</strong> We are developing tools and methods that help participants identify harms, reason about fairness trade-offs, and make judgments about AI systems in context-sensitive ways.
    </p>

    <h4>Selected Publications</h4>
    <ul>
      <li>Beyond Bias Detection: Non-Expert Auditors and Normative Reasoning in AI Oversight</li>
      <li>Emerging Practices in Participatory AI Design in Public Sector Innovation</li>
      <li>Re-imagining Fairness in Machine Learning</li>
    </ul>

  <div class="research-project-text">
    <h3>Bridging Civic Data Divides</h3>
    <p>
      <strong>What it is:</strong> This project examines why participation in civic data systems is uneven, especially for marginalized communities. I use a sequential model of access to study how motivation, material resources, skills, and opportunities for use combine to shape who can participate in data-driven governance.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>Why it matters:</strong> Access to data is not just about making information available. This work shows how structural barriers continue to shape who can interpret, use, and benefit from civic data, and helps identify design interventions that could make data systems more equitable.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>What we are building and studying:</strong> This research develops conceptual and empirical models of exclusion in civic data systems and proposes ways of designing for broader, more meaningful participation in public decision-making.
    </p>

<div class="research-project">
  <div class="research-project-text">
    <h3>The Knowledge Map</h3>
    <p>
      <strong>What it is:</strong> The Knowledge Map is a co-designed data-reporting portal developed with community-based organizations. It combines official indicators with community stories and place-based knowledge so that planning processes can better reflect lived experience, especially in disadvantaged communities.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>Why it matters:</strong> Public data systems often fail to capture the kinds of local knowledge that residents use to understand environmental harms, infrastructure conditions, and neighborhood priorities. This project explores how storytelling, community annotation, and official indicators can be brought together in more just and representative ways.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>What we are building and studying:</strong> We are designing a place-based portal that integrates community narratives with governmental indicators in order to support climate and infrastructure planning that is more responsive to local experience.
    </p>

    <h4>Selected Publications</h4>

      <li>Paper on environmental perceptions and institutional indicators</li>

<div class="research-project">
  <div class="research-project-text">
    <h3>Language Socialization and Retention</h3>
    <p>
      <strong>What it is:</strong> This project studies how newcomers learn the language of online collaborative communities and how that process shapes long-term participation. Using natural language processing and sequence analysis, I examine whether adopting specialized community vocabulary helps people become recognized as insiders and remain engaged over time.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>Why it matters:</strong> Language is one of the ways communities signal belonging, expertise, and participation. This work helps explain how socialization happens in distributed online systems and how community language can support or hinder newcomer retention.
    </p>
    <p>
      <strong>What we are building and studying:</strong> We are developing analytical approaches for identifying linguistic assimilation, tracing how it unfolds across time, and studying how it relates to participation, integration, and learning.
    </p>

    <h4>Selected Publications</h4>
    <ul>
      <li>Please Say “Shibboleth”: Socialization Through Language Adoption in Virtual Citizen Science</li>
      <li>Linguistic Styles Across Time: The Lexical Entrainment of Citizen Scientists</li>
      <li>Linguistic adoption in online citizen science: A structurational perspective</li>
